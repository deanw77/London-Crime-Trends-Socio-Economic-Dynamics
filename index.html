London Crime Trends


We first take a brief look at the distribution of crime across the London Boroughs


We can see that central London is a hotspot for crime. This largely due to much higher rates of theft in central London. In fact theft Accounts for over 50% of all crime in Central London. The next most common crime is violence against the person, which accounts for around 20% of all crime in Central London.


If we filter by Burglary and Violence we see a much more even distribution across the London Boroughs

2012

In August 2011, London experienced a significant crime spike and widespread civil unrest, including riots that involved looting, arson, and violence. The unrest was triggered by the police shooting of Mark Duggan in Tottenham, London. During the five days of disorder, more than 5,000 crimes were committed, mainly burglary, criminal damage, and violence against persons, with five fatalities according to the Royal Economic Society and the Home Office. In the affected areas, crime rose by 57% during August.

2019

In 2019, police-recorded vehicle offences in London increased, continuing a trend seen over the previous three years. While the increase was lower than in previous years, it is believed that this reflects genuine increases in vehicle crime rather than just improved recording practices. Specifically, the subcategory "theft or unauthorized taking of a motor vehicle" saw an 8% increase, while "theft from a vehicle" increased by 1%

Theft Drop

Phone technology, including features like activation lock, theft detection lock, and remote tracking, is playing a role in reducing street theft by making stolen phones less desirable to thieves. These features, especially when used in conjunction with proactive measures like setting up strong passwords and using security apps, can help deter theft and aid in the recovery of stolen devices

Crime and Unemployment


Deep dive to how unemployment rates effect both the total crime and the types of crime.


...we can see that there is a strong correlation between unemployment rates and crime rates. As unemployment rates increase, so do crime rates. This is particularly true for theft and burglary, which are the most common types of crime in London.
